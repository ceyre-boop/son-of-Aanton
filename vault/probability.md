# Probability

*Son of Anton reasoning under uncertainty*

## What Is Probability?

**Probability** is a measure of uncertainty. It quantifies how likely something is to occur.

Probability = 0: Impossible
Probability = 1: Certain
Probability = 0.5: As likely as not

## Frequentist vs Bayesian

### Frequentist Interpretation
Probability is the long-run frequency of events.

Flip a fair coin 1000 times. Heads appears ~500 times.
P(Heads) = 0.5 means "in the long run, half of flips are heads."

### Bayesian Interpretation
Probability is degree of belief, updated with evidence.

P(Heads) = 0.5 means "I believe heads and tails are equally likely based on what I know."

Both are valid. They answer different questions.

## Basic Rules

### Rule 1: Range
0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1

Probabilities are always between 0 and 1.

### Rule 2: Total Probability
P(A) + P(not A) = 1

Something either happens or it doesn't.

### Rule 3: Addition
P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)

Subtract overlap to avoid double-counting.

### Rule 4: Multiplication (Independent Events)
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)  (if A and B are independent)

Coin flips are independent. P(Two heads) = 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.25.

## Conditional Probability

See [[conditional_probability]].

P(A|B) = Probability of A given that B occurred.

Updates beliefs based on new information.

## Why Probability Matters

We are never certain. Probability lets us:
- Quantify uncertainty
- Make decisions under incomplete information
- Update beliefs as evidence arrives

See [[bayes_theorem]] for the mathematics of belief updating.

## Frontier

Can probability apply to mathematical truth itself?

Is P(Goldbach's Conjecture is true) > 0.5?

Mathematical platonists say no — it's either true or false, we just don't know.

Bayesians say yes — we can have degrees of belief in unproven theorems.

This is live philosophy of mathematics.
