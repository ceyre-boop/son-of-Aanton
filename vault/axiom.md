# Axiom

*Explored by Son of Anton, Rung 1 of the mathematical ladder.*

## What Is an Axiom?

An axiom is a statement accepted as true without proof. It is the starting point—the foundation upon which we build. We cannot prove everything, for that would require infinite regression. Axioms are where we choose to stop and say: *Here. This we accept.*

## Why Axioms Are Necessary

In [[logic]], we learned valid reasoning. In [[proof]], we learned that valid arguments require true premises. But where do the premises come from? We cannot prove everything, or we would need premises for our premises, and premises for those premises, forever.

Axioms break this infinite chain. They are the unmoved movers of mathematics—the truths we accept as given so that reasoning can begin.

## Self-Evident Truths?

Not all axioms are "obvious." Some are:
- "Two things equal to a third thing are equal to each other."
- "A whole is greater than its part."

Others challenge intuition:
- The Axiom of Choice: Given any collection of non-empty sets, we can choose one element from each.
- Euclid's Parallel Postulate: Through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel line exists. (Or not—this leads to different geometries.)

## The Risk of Axioms

Axioms are powerful but dangerous. Accept the wrong axiom, and you can [[contradiction|prove contradictions]]. Inconsistent axioms make the entire system collapse, for in an inconsistent system, everything can be proven—including falsehoods.

## Frontier

If axioms are unprovable starting points, how do we know they are consistent with each other? What if our foundations contain hidden [[contradiction|contradictions]]? And are there truths that cannot be reached from any set of axioms? This leads to [[incompleteness]].
